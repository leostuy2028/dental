Reference for reading a panoramic dental radiograph (OPG). Use this knowledge and read the whole image systematically rather than fixating on one area.

TOOTH NUMBERING (FDI two-digit system, ISO-3950). Every tooth has a two-digit code. The first digit is the quadrant, using the patient's own right and left: 1 = upper right, 2 = upper left, 3 = lower left, 4 = lower right (permanent teeth). The second digit is the position counting from the midline outward: 1 central incisor, 2 lateral incisor, 3 canine, 4 first premolar, 5 second premolar, 6 first molar, 7 second molar, 8 third molar. So #11 is the upper-right central incisor, #46 is the lower-right first molar, and #38 is the lower-left third molar.

WHAT THE OPG SHOWS, AND ITS BLIND SPOTS. A single flat (2D) image captures, in one exposure, all the teeth, the mandible, parts of the maxilla including much of the maxillary sinus, the hard palate, and both temporomandibular joints (TMJs). Only structures lying inside a narrow in-focus zone, the focal trough (about 20 mm wide in the side regions and about 10 mm in front), appear sharp; anything outside it is blurred or invisible. As a result, lesions and foreign bodies outside the focal trough are under-detected, and the OPG is unreliable for maxillary-sinus disease and midfacial fractures. Absence of a finding on an OPG does not rule it out.

DO NOT MISTAKE SHADOWS OR ARTEFACTS FOR DISEASE. Because of the rotational projection, structures near the edges but outside the focal plane cast "ghost" shadows that appear on the opposite side, higher up, and magnified; structures near the midline outside the trough cast double real shadows. Ghost and double shadows of normal anatomy (the opposite side of the mandible, the hard palate, the spine) frequently overlie the image and mimic findings. A pseudocyst of the mandibular condyle (a normal concavity) can look like a cyst. If the tongue is not pressed against the hard palate, a radiolucent air shadow darkens the periapical regions of the upper teeth and can hide or imitate a periapical lesion. Horizontal distortion is greater than vertical, so the OPG is not reliable for horizontal measurements.

READ EVERY STRUCTURE IN ORDER. Work through, in turn: (a) which teeth are present versus missing, plus any supernumerary or impacted teeth; (b) dental implants, counted separately from natural teeth; (c) caries, seen as radiolucencies reaching the dentin, on the proximal or occlusal surfaces; (d) fillings and other restorations, distinguishing simple fillings from crowns and fixed prostheses (restorations record the patient's treatment history); (e) root-canal fillings; (f) apical (periapical) health at each root tip; (g) the alveolar bone level, judged relative to root length in thirds (coronal, middle, apical), where a healthy bone crest sits about 2 mm below the cemento-enamel junction.

PERIAPICAL LESIONS (the hardest and highest-value finding). They present as periapical radiolucencies (dark areas) at or near a tooth-root apex and are usually asymptomatic, found incidentally. Most periapical radiolucencies (about 78%) are due to apical periodontitis, inflammation spreading from the root canal, but not every periapical radiolucency is inflammatory; the differential includes both odontogenic and non-odontogenic lesions. Detecting them on an OPG is limited: a lesion needs roughly 30 to 50% bone-mineral loss before it shows at all, OPG sensitivity is only about 28 to 49% and drops sharply for lesions smaller than about 4.5 mm, and superimposition and 2D distortion hide small lesions. Judge from the signs that are visible, and stay appropriately cautious about ruling a lesion out.
